28 "Sequential and Parallel Solution-Biased Search for Subgraph Algorithms":

30 https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-19212-9_2

35
36 The idea is, rather than always mapping into the target vertex with largest

38 This is done simply by choosing a random vertex,

40 so that lower degrees are less likely.

44 /* 2^{-5} = 1/32 ~ 0.03. A choice with probability <3% is probably
45 not very significant for the overall search.
46
47 The probability of picking a vertex (the mass) is proportional to
48 2^degree.
49
50 Therefore if a vertex has degree <= max degree - 5, then it has weighting
51 <= 1/32 ~ 3% of any vertex of maximum degree.
52
53 Thus, including these vertices also would usually only make a difference
54 in a very small number of cases.
55
56 As explained in the linked paper, the heuristic generally chooses
57 highest degree vertices first, but occasionally chooses
58 slightly lower degrees.
59
60 This is based on a very rough estimate that the expected number of
61 solutions in the subtree is roughly C . 2^degree.
62
63 However, this is all very rough and experimental (as the paper says);
64 a better estimate is more like A.B^degree for unknown B>1, but that's
65 still very rough.
66
67 As the paper says, it generally gives better results than non-exponential
68 formulae but there's no deep underlying theory here.
69
70 The selection "5" is also an arbitrary choice; we need a cutoff to avoid
71 wasting time on vanishingly unlikely choices, and 3% seems small enough
72 that it's a reasonable cutoff. (OK, we could have 1 vertex of degree 10
73 and 1000 vertices of degree 5. But even in that case, I think we'd rather
74 just try the degree 10 vertex first).
75 */
